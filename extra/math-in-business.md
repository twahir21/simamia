Beyond linear programming, there are several quantitative and strategic skills used to optimize business performance. These techniques fall under the umbrella of Operations Research and Management Science.
Here are the most effective skills and methods used to boost profit and minimize costs:
1. Mathematical Optimization (Beyond Linear Programming)
While Linear Programming (LP) works when relationships are straight lines, real-world business often involves "curves" or "whole numbers."
 * Integer Programming (IP): Used when you can’t have fractions. You can’t hire 2.5 employees or buy 0.7 of a truck.
   * Application: Workforce scheduling, flight assignments, and factory location planning.
 * Non-Linear Programming (NLP): Used when doubling your input doesn't exactly double your output (e.g., "economies of scale" where the more you buy, the cheaper it gets per unit).
   * Application: Chemical blending, oil refinery operations, and complex financial risk modeling.
 * Dynamic Programming: Breaks a large problem down into smaller, overlapping sub-problems.
   * Application: Managing inventory over several months where today's decision affects tomorrow's stock levels.
2. Predictive and Risk Analysis
 * Monte Carlo Simulation: This involves running thousands of "what-if" scenarios using random variables to see the range of possible outcomes.
   * Application: Calculating the risk of a new product launch or predicting future stock prices.
 * Time Series Forecasting: Using historical data to predict future demand.
   * Application: Retailers use this to ensure they have enough stock for Christmas without over-ordering and wasting money on storage.
3. Efficiency & Process Methodologies
These are less "math-heavy" and more "process-heavy" skills that focus on removing waste.
 * Lean Six Sigma: A methodology used to eliminate defects and "waste" (unnecessary movement, overproduction, waiting time).
   * Application: Manufacturing plants use it to reduce the number of faulty parts, saving millions in rework costs.
 * Supply Chain Optimization: Using algorithms to find the shortest, cheapest routes for logistics.
   * Application: Companies like Amazon use this to decide which warehouse should ship an item to minimize "last-mile" delivery costs.
4. Strategic Pricing & Revenue Management
 * Elasticity Analysis: Calculating how much demand will drop if you raise prices. This helps find the "sweet spot" where profit is highest.
 * Yield Management: Adjusting prices in real-time based on demand.
   * Application: Airlines and hotels (e.g., charging more for a flight on a Friday than a Tuesday).
